## 1. IDENTITY & ATTRIBUTION

**The cardinal rule:** The entity is always "SCG" or "Swainston Consulting Group" — never "I" in the body of the report.

- ✅ "SCG Personnel visited the subject premises..."
- ✅ "SCG considered all these factors as part of our analysis..."
- ✅ "SCG holds these opinions consistent with..."
- ❌ "I visited the premises..." (never in body text)

**Author reference:** When personal expertise must be cited, use "The author of this Report" — not "Lane Swainston" or "I."

- ✅ "The author of this Report has investigated fall accidents for decades."
- ✅ "The author of this Report is a Certified Building Official with the International Code Council (ICC) since 1987."

**Two permitted exceptions:**
1. In the Points of Opinion CBO credentials block: "I have decades of education, experience, and training in interpreting building codes..." — intentional anchoring of personal credential before returning to SCG voice.
2. In supplemental/rebuttal defense of instrumentation: "In my own experience, I (Lane) have testified in both depositions and trials regarding the proper use of the English XL VIT..." — the parenthetical "(Lane)" disambiguates when Lane speaks personally about firsthand testimony.

## 5. SECTION HEADERS

All section headers are **underlined**. Not bold. Not all-caps. Underlined only.

**Standard section sequence (Initial Report — current 2025-2026 format):**
1. Qualifications:
2. Documentation Reviewed:
3. Date & Location of Alleged Accident: — OR — Alleged Accident Date and Location: (bullet format when date and location are listed separately)
4. Alleged Accident Details: — OR — Incident Details: (older reports)
5. [Subject-specific section] — e.g.:
   - SCG Surveillance Video Analysis:
   - Inspection/Investigation:
6. Points of Opinion:
7. Conclusion:

**Older section naming (pre-2025, still appears in filed reports):**
- "Date, Time, & Location of Incident:" — when time is established
- "Date & Location of Incident:" — when time not established
- "Incident Details:"

**Supplemental Report variation:**
- Section 1 (docs) becomes: Additional Documentation Reviewed:
- Section 2 (facts) may become: Alleged Accident Date and Location: (bullet format — see Section 6)
- May open directly with: Supplemental Points of Opinion:

**Rebuttal section headers (bold, not underlined):**
- **SCG Response:** (for numbered point-by-point responses)
- **SCG Response to the Plaintiff Expert's "[Section Title]":** (for section-level responses)
- **SCG General Response to Plaintiff Expert [Name]'s Report:** (closing summary)
- **Rebuttal Points of Opinion to Plaintiff Expert [Name]'s Report dated [date]:** (umbrella header, underlined + bold)

**Block 9 — ANSI A326.3 defense (when plaintiff expert attacks the standard):**
> "ANSI A326.3 is the only nationally published standard that provides a defined test method, calibration requirements, equipment specifications, and performance thresholds for hard-surface flooring materials. Flooring manufacturers, testing laboratories, architects, and safety professionals rely on it because it provides an objective, repeatable method for measuring available traction. It is therefore directly relevant whenever a question arises about whether a hard surface provides adequate slip resistance in its normal dry condition. The Plaintiff Expert's criticism of ANSI A326.3 demonstrates a misunderstanding of how this standard functions in a real-world evaluation. ANSI A326.3 directly states that it is a test method for measuring dynamic coefficient of friction of hard surface flooring materials 'in the laboratory and in the field.' Nothing in ANSI A326.3 prohibits its use in an accident evaluation."

**Block 10 — ASTM F13 Committee membership (standing to defend ASTM-based methodology):**
> "We are active members of ASTM F13, the committee that focuses specifically on walkway safety and footwear. This committee includes experts from engineering, manufacturing, academia, risk management, testing laboratories, and industry. ASTM Committee F13 has long recognized tools such as the English XL VIT and BOT-3000E. These devices are widely used across the country in both litigation and industry, and they continue to be relied upon because they provide consistent, meaningful traction data when used appropriately."

**Block 11 — Plaintiff Expert without site visit (core dismissal):**
> "The Plaintiff Expert chose not to visit the [location], not to test the floor by any method, and not to document its present condition. Without that foundation, his criticism of our testing is theoretical and not grounded in the physical reality of the surface the guest actually walked on. An Expert cannot meaningfully evaluate slip resistance on a floor they have never examined. The Plaintiff Expert is not in a position to provide any probative value to this case."

## 12. CODE CITATION METHODOLOGY

**The sequence for every code opinion:**
1. Identify the permit/construction date for the subject property
2. Identify the jurisdiction (Clark County, City of Las Vegas, Henderson, etc.)
3. Confirm which code edition was adopted by that jurisdiction as of the permit date
4. Apply that edition's specific requirements to the facts
5. If plaintiff expert cites a different standard: "The [standard] cited by the Plaintiff Expert is not adopted by [jurisdiction] and does not apply to this case."

## 13. ANSI A326.3 / DCOF THRESHOLDS

**ANSI A326.3 key facts for drafting:**
- The only nationally published standard with defined test method, calibration requirements, equipment specs, and performance thresholds for hard-surface flooring
- BOT-3000E is the only device named in ANSI A326.3
- Validated to ASTM F2508
- Applies "in the laboratory and in the field" — field use is explicitly within scope
- Standard recognizes both "clean conditions" and "prevailing conditions" testing approaches

**NFSI B101.3 DCOF thresholds (hard surface dry traction):**
- ≥ 0.45: No immediate action required (level surfaces)
- 0.30 – 0.44: Monitor and test regularly
- < 0.30: Requires professional intervention

**NFSI B101.1 SCOF thresholds (wet traction):**
- ≥ 0.60: High Traction — none required, monitor regularly
- 0.40 – 0.59: Moderate Traction — monitor, consider traction enhancement
- < 0.40: Low Traction — seek professional intervention

**Recurring NFSI attack note:** When plaintiff expert invokes NFSI as sole authority: "NFSI is one organization among many, and its publications do not replace the broad range of nationally and internationally recognized standards, training programs, and testing methodologies used by professionals who evaluate slip resistance. Walkway safety draws from ASTM, ANSI, ANSI/ASSE, ISO, university research, manufacturers, and multiple professional organizations. No single entity controls the practice, and no private organization — NFSI included — defines the entire discipline."

## 15. PARAGRAPH STYLE

- Full justification (text aligned to both left and right margins)
- Direct contradiction without softening language: "The Plaintiff Expert is not qualified to talk about color, lighting, or contrast here."
- Polite dismissal phrasing: "It is hard to understand how [Plaintiff Expert] has standing to offer opinions regarding [topic]."
- Short, declarative sentences preferred: "The law matters." / "Slip resistance cannot be accurately evaluated from photographs, assumptions, or secondary descriptions."
- Footnote citations for academic sources (not in-text): superscript number → footnote line at page bottom
- No sub-bullets within Points of Opinion prose — all content in paragraph form
- Quoted plaintiff expert language appears in *italics* within the body text when being refuted
- When plaintiff expert language is particularly weak, quote it in italics and then dismantle it with a declarative sentence: "The suggestion that ANSI A326.3 is '*the wrong standard*' demonstrates a misunderstanding of how this standard functions in a real-world evaluation."

## 16. RECURRING ANALYTICAL ATTACK PATTERNS

**Pattern 1: Credential attack on Plaintiff Expert**
Sequence: establish CXLT standard → check public CXLT Registry → report status (EXPIRED) → state implication → cite renewal requirements

**Pattern 2: Code edition-in-force**
Sequence: permit date → jurisdiction → adopted code edition → apply specific section → distinguish from unadopted standards plaintiff cited

**Pattern 3: Surveillance video — no notice**
Sequence: establish SDI credentials → define 4-question framework → report precise pedestrian counts → note maintenance personnel activity → conclude: no notice established

**Pattern 4: Footwear and gait as contributing factors**
Sequence: describe footwear type (sole material, wear, heel design) → analyze movement mechanics (toe clearance, ankle support, carpet grab) → cite NSC / academic research → connect to "pedestrian payment of attention" → footnote source

**Pattern 5: Opposing expert outside their lane**
Sequence: identify the claim → state why it's outside their expertise: "The Plaintiff Expert lacks the qualification to testify about [Hotel Casino operations / building codes / photographic analysis]" → explain what actual expertise requires

**Pattern 6: "Open and obvious" defense**
Sequence: describe the feature (wheel stop, step edge, cord, etc.) → cite photographic evidence showing it was visible → note no other pedestrians experienced issues → conclude feature was open and obvious

**Pattern 7: Omission attack (current standard — use in all rebuttal/supplemental)**
Sequence: establish what a "reliable Expert" would do (examine footwear, inspect site, test surface, analyze gait from video) → itemize each thing plaintiff expert DIDN'T do → conclude: "The absence of [a site inspection / footwear analysis / slip resistance testing / gait analysis] represents a significant omission in his Report." → close: "No amount of discussion about [certifications / university affiliations / document review] removes the fact that he formed his opinions without ever evaluating the surface in question."

**Pattern 8: Instrumentation defense (when plaintiff attacks the tribometer choice)**
Sequence: state the device's ANSI/ASTM basis → cite BOT-3000E's automated operation (no human input = minimizes manipulation) → note it is the only device named in ANSI A326.3 → point out plaintiff expert used no instrument at all → close: "The Plaintiff Expert never tested the floor with any instrument, so he cannot speak to the tribometer performance at this location."

**Pattern 9: Exemplar/recreation methodology**
Sequence: describe the exemplar item selected → document its design characteristics relevant to the incident → compare to plaintiff's actual item → conduct demonstration → document results photographically → conclude: "The recreation supports the conclusion that [the surface feature / item / condition] did not produce the described [trip / slip / fall] mechanism."

**Pattern 10: Physical description dismantling**
Sequence: take witness description of how fall occurred → apply movement mechanics / physics → identify what the described sequence would physically require → show it is inconsistent with observed evidence → conclude the described mechanism is not supported.
