How It Works

Four agents. Built. Each one knows its job.

You give BlackBar a matter — the attorney, the case caption, the incident facts, the report type, the documents in hand. Four agents take it from there and hand you back a reviewable draft.

✓ Built
📋

1. Intake Agent

Accepts the matter in any format — forwarded email, voice memo, bullet list. Normalizes everything into a structured brief. Flags missing fields. Won't hand off until the case is complete.

✓ Built
🔍

2. Research Agent

Resolves every research flag from intake: CXLT Registry status for opposing experts, code edition in force as of the permit date, the full Peterson playbook if Peterson appears, boilerplate block selection, section plan.

✓ Built
✍️

3. Drafting Agent

Builds the full report in your voice against VOICE.md. Inserts boilerplate verbatim — no paraphrasing, no "improvement." Uses Gleason, Heagy, or Anderson as the benchmark depending on report type.

✓ Built
✅

4. QA Agent

Runs 10 specific checks before you see the draft: date format, "SCG" not "I," "alleged" language, boilerplate integrity, conclusion verbatim, benchmark comparison by section. Scores each section Pass / Review / Fail.

Three explicit checkpoints before anything is finalized: after intake (confirm the matter is set up correctly), after the research summary (confirm facts are accurate), and after the draft (review and approve or redline). Nothing is saved as final without your signoff.

One More Thing

BlackBar does not write opinions. You do.

Worth saying plainly: BlackBar drafts structure and language around the opinions you provide. It will not generate "The floor was not maintained in a reasonably safe condition" or "The Plaintiff Expert's methodology is unreliable" on its own. Those are your opinions. They come from your site visit, your analysis, your 30 years of walking into Clark County courtrooms.

What BlackBar handles is everything around that: the section headers, the boilerplate blocks, the documentation list, the figure captions, the date format, the CXLT registry check, the conclusion paragraph, the credential logo block, the Peterson playbook when Peterson shows up again. The parts that take time but do not require your expertise. The parts that, if done wrong, undermine the credibility of the parts that do.

When this works, you walk into the drafting step with your notes in hand and walk out with a document that reads like SCG filed it — because it was built from the same language SCG has always used. Your judgment. Your credentials. Your voice. Faster to produce. Easier to review. The same report that has held up for 30 years.
